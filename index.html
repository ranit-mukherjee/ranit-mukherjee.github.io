Prologue

Through experiments on droplets, frost, fluid interfaces, and granular materials, I try to understand how simple physical interactions drive complex phenomena in nature and engineering applications. My goal is to design analogs of complex natural systems and processes and probe them for fundamental physical understanding.

I have a PhD from Virginia Tech, USA with Prof. Jonathan Boreyko and a postdoctoral experience with Prof. Sungyon Lee at the University of Minnesota, Twin Cities.

My research draws inspiration from technological challenges, biological systems, and ecological questions. I work with collaborators across disciplines, from plant pathologists uncovering how jumping dew droplets spread disease to engineers at Rolls-Royce developing new de-icing strategies. Explore the Research Stories below to see how these collaborations come to life.

Research Stories

Lift off and crashing back: the surprising end of jumping-droplet condensation

In any heat transfer devices, the condenser surface inevitably accumulates water droplets. Removing this water is an engineering challenge as they are a hinderence to efficient heat transfer. On regular metal surfaces, this is usually achieved automatically as individual droplets of water merge and become too large to fall off the surface via gravity. However, this only works when the surface is not horizontal. If the surface is superhydrophobic, the extremely low adhesion between the droplet base and the surface ensures that the energy released during merging is partly converted into an upward velocity. This is the basis of jumping-droplet condensation, the newest and most efficient condensation modeN. Miljkovic, Nano Letters, 2012 till date.

Jumped droplets may return on an upside down surface, being caught in the incoming vapor stream.However, if the structured surface has defects, droplets can get pinned which would reduce the rate of jumping. While some of the jumped droplets eventually return to the surface in most cases, if the jump rate is lower than the return rate, we would eventually see flooding. Far more counter-intuitively, upside-down surfaces too, can exhibit droplet return due to vapor flow entrainment near the surface. Our experiments show that for a condensation period of 3 hrs or more, the inclined surfaces perform far better than horizontal surfaces thanks to the assistance from the gravitational departure.
