Prologue

Through experiments on droplets, frost, fluid interfaces, and granular materials, I try to understand how simple physical interactions drive complex phenomena in nature and engineering applications. My goal is to design analogs of complex natural systems and processes and probe them for fundamental physical understanding.

I have a PhD from Virginia Tech, USA with Prof. Jonathan Boreyko and a postdoctoral experience with Prof. Sungyon Lee at the University of Minnesota, Twin Cities.

My research draws inspiration from technological challenges, biological systems, and ecological questions. I work with collaborators across disciplines, from plant pathologists uncovering how jumping dew droplets spread disease to engineers at Rolls-Royce developing new de-icing strategies. Explore the Research Stories below to see how these collaborations come to life.

Lift off and crashing back: the surprising end of jumping-droplet condensation

In any heat transfer devices, the condenser surface inevitably accumulates water droplets. Removing this water is an engineering challenge as they are a hinderence to efficient heat transfer. On regular metal surfaces, this is usually achieved automatically as individual droplets of water merge and become too large to fall off the surface via gravity. However, this only works when the surface is not horizontal. If the surface is superhydrophobic, the extremely low adhesion between the droplet base and the surface ensures that the energy released during merging is partly converted into an upward velocity. This is the basis of jumping-droplet condensation, the newest and most efficient mode of dropwise condensation till date.
