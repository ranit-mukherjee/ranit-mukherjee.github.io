Condensation and Evaporation

Effect of Surface Orientation on Jumping Droplet-Condensation

When two or more micrometric droplets merge on a superhydrophobic substrate, they jump out of plane. 10 years after its discovery (by my thesis advisor when he was a grad student), we now know that jumping droplet-condensation is thermally more efficient than regular dropwise condensation. While the main focus of most of the research on this topic has been on tuning the micro and nanoscale geometries of the surface, one important factor was the orientation of the substrate itself. As such out-of-plane jumping departures of the droplets does not depend on the gravitational orientation, it was easy to overlook or even claim surface orientation will not have any significant effect on the long term heat transfer efficiency for the jumping droplet condensation. Here we have shown through extensive experiments and theoretical analysis that even the jumping droplet-condensation is not immune to gravity if condensation is run for longer periods.

How Rust Disease in Wheat is related to Jumping Droplet-Condensation?

Jumping-droplet condensation of water is seen mainly on hierarchical superhydrophobic surface due to the low adhesion and very low contact angle of water on these surfaces. Wheat leaves also have such micro and nanostructures making them superhydrophobic in nature. Our lab has previously discovered that when natural dew forms on the wheat leaves, the jumped condensates from the leaf can carry the micrometric spore particles. The implication of this study being much like rain-splash and wind, dew can also help in pathogen spreading among plants. As a companion piece to this previous study, we want to know the horizontal extent of spreading possible by jumping droplet condensation alone and also with the help of wind. This is the first such study which shows that even without significant wind, jumping droplet-condensation can easily spread rust pathogens in a canopy (Manuscript under preparation).

A Thermal Diode Based on Bridging of Condensate Droplets

Thermal diodes are devices used to transport heat in preferentially one direction. Phase-change thermal diodes have greater thermal efficiency compared to other thermal diodes due to the involvement of latent heat. But they generally suffer from either geometric constraints (1D) or dependence on gravity or low durability (e.g., jumping-droplet thermal diode). Here, a novel bridging-droplet thermal diode is proposed which is simple in design, durable and efficient with maximum orientation-independent heat transfer ratio reaching ~80%.

Liquid-Infused Surfaces

Liquid-Infused Surfaces (LIS) or Slippery Liquid-Infused Surfaces (SLIPS) are the super-repellent substrates we deserved but did not get until 2011. They are more slippery (Contact Angle Hysteresis less than 1°) and much more durablethan regular superhydrophobic surfaces. These surfaces made with infusing certain lubricating liquids within the pores of a textured (micro/nano/hierarchical) surface. The choice of lubricating liquid is dependent on the liquid we need to displace and also on the surface chemistry. They have been shown to repel almost any kind of fouling materials, from bacteria to ketchup. In general, the lubricant liquid is hold either by some textures or some silicone or fluorine-based absorbent polymer layers on the substrate. The inherent problem with such approach is that these coatings are expensive and the durability of the final product will depend on the durability of adhesion between the coating and the substrate. Here we have used common hydrocarbon-based polymers used in food packaging such as polyethylene and polypropylene and infused them with compatible oils to create an inexpensive, easily obtainable super-repellant coating.

Frost

If you wring a glass rod with a piece of cotton and bring it near a thin stream of water, the stream is deflected away from the rod. There are multiple explanation (claiming polar properties of the liquid stream as the reason and some refuting this claim) for this simple experiment we probably all did as a child. Something similar happened when we brought water near a subsrate covered with frost in our laboratory: few pieces of frost dendrites broke off and shot towards the water. Clearly as excited as children, we decided to continue experimenting and find an explanation for this. Thus came the "Jumping Frost" project (under revision).
