Condensation and Evaporation

Effect of Surface Orientation on Jumping Droplet-Condensation

When two or more micrometric droplets condensing on a superhydrophobic substrate merge, the coalesced droplet jump out of plane. 10 years after its discovery, we now know that such jumping droplet-condensation is thermally more efficient than regular dropwise condensation. The main focus of most of the research on this topic has been on tuning the micro and nanoscale geometries of the surface to decrease the minimum droplet size of departure or allowing for cascading removal. As such out-of-plane jumping departures of the droplets is independent of the gravitational orientation of the condensing surface, it has never been formally studied. Here we have shown through extensive experiments and theoretical analysis that even the jumping droplet-condensation is not immune to gravity if condensation is run for longer periods. The result is degradation of heat transfer efficiency with time, depending on the surface orientation. Link for paper

How Rust Disease in Wheat is related to Jumping Droplet-Condensation?

Wheat leaves have micro and nanostructures rendering them superhydrophobic with a very low hysteresis (about 4°). Our lab has previously discovered that when dew droplets form on the wheat leaves, coalescence of these droplets can launch them out of the leaf plane. For a diseased leaf (Wheat rust, P. triticina), the jumped droplets can also carry the micrometric spores. This, in turn, can spread the disease between plants if the horizontal range of the spore-laden droplets are enough. To find to what extent jumping-droplet condensation contribute to disease spreading in wheat, we performed condensation experiments with and without wind. If the wind speed is just enough, it can significantly increase the range of a spore-laden jumped droplet without affecting the dew formation on the leaves. This establishes condensation as an abiotic disease spreading mechanism along with wind and rain splash (Manuscript under preparation).

A Thermal Diode Based on Bridging of Condensate Droplets

Thermal diodes are devices used to transport heat in preferentially one direction. Phase-change thermal diodes have greater thermal efficiency compared to other thermal diodes due to the involvement of latent heat. But they generally suffer from either geometric constraints (1D) or dependence on gravity or low durability (e.g., jumping-droplet thermal diode). Here, a novel bridging-droplet thermal diode is proposed which is simple in design, durable and efficient with maximum orientation-independent heat transfer ratio reaching ~80%. Link to paper

Liquid-Infused Surfaces

Liquid-Infused Surfaces (LIS) or Slippery Liquid-Infused Surfaces (SLIPS) are super-repellent substrates 2011 which are more slippery (Contact Angle Hysteresis less than 1°) and much more durable than regular superhydrophobic surfaces. These surfaces are made by infusing chemically compatible lubricating liquids within the pores of a textured surface. The current methods of making slippery surfaces out of any material is coating the surface with an absorbent/textured polymer. While this method is successful, it suffers from several problems- a) the polymer coatings are often expensive and b) the assembly process is also complex. Here we have used common hydrocarbon-based polymers used in food packaging (e.g., polyethylene, polypropylene) and infused them with compatible oils to create an inexpensive, easily obtainable super-repellant coating. Frost

If you wring a glass rod with a piece of cotton and bring it near a thin stream of water, the stream is deflected away from the rod. There are multiple explanations (claiming polar properties of the liquid stream as the reason and some refuting this claim) for this simple experiment we probably all did as a child. Something similar happened when we brought water near a subsrate covered with frost in our laboratory: few pieces of frost dendrites broke off and shot towards the water. Our high-speed photography and numerical analysis suggests this is due to a temperature gradient-induced charge separation process which occurs in ice (or frost). The charge separated frost develops negative charges on the warmer ends which in turn polarizes and induces opposite charges on the water film (or droplet). The next step is to use this remarkable property of ice for developing a novel de-icing device (Just accepted in ACS Nano).
